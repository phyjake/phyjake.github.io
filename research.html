Current Work

Atomic Structure of Xenon

AMO Physics Research Group · CU Denver · Ongoing

My current project involves modeling the atomic structure of xenon using the Dirac-Hartree-Fock method. This is a relativistic approach to the many-electron problem — in heavy atoms like xenon, electrons move fast enough that you can't ignore special relativity. The non-relativistic Schrödinger equation isn't enough; you need the Dirac equation, which naturally incorporates spin and relativistic kinematics.

The "Hartree-Fock" part means we're using a self-consistent field approach — each electron sees an average potential from all the others, and you iterate until the solution converges. It's computationally intensive and conceptually rich. I'm learning a lot about the gap between the physics we write on a chalkboard and the physics we can actually compute.

Why Xenon?

Xenon has 54 electrons. That's enough that relativistic effects genuinely matter, but not so many that the computation becomes impossibly expensive. It sits in a sweet spot for testing methods.

Presentations

Science as a Mechanism for Cultural Evolution

Western Regional Honors Conference · 2025

This talk explored the idea that science doesn't just describe the world — it actively shapes what a culture considers "true." Scientific frameworks become lenses through which we see everything else, and the history of science is partly a history of these shifts in collective understanding.

It was my first time presenting original intellectual work to an audience outside my university, and it clarified a lot about how I think about the relationship between physics and philosophy.

Where I'm Headed

Long-term, I want to do theoretical physics. The kind of work that sits at the boundary between physics and mathematics — where you're not just applying known techniques but developing new frameworks for understanding.

I know that's a long road. Right now I'm focused on building the mathematical and physical intuition that makes that kind of thinking possible. Every course, every problem set, every conversation is part of that.
