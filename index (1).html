Jake Hooning

I'm an undergraduate studying physics and mathematics at the University of Colorado Denver. I live in Evergreen, Colorado.

The thing that pulled me into physics wasn't any particular equation or experiment — it was the realization that the questions I'd always been asking (what is space? what is time? why does anything exist at all?) were questions that physics actually tries to answer. Not with certainty, but with care.

Right now I'm working in an atomic physics research group, modeling the structure of xenon using relativistic quantum methods. I'm also a learning assistant for Physics 2, which means I spend a fair amount of time helping people untangle circuits and make sense of Gauss's law. Teaching has been one of the best ways I've found to understand something deeply — you can't explain what you don't actually know.

I'm trying to build a foundation for theoretical work. That means a double major in physics and math, and a lot of time sitting with ideas until they make sense from the ground up — not just memorizing the procedure, but understanding why it works.

Outside of all that, I'm a member of the Society of Physics Students and the American Physical Society. In 2025, I gave a talk at the Western Regional Honors Conference about science as a mechanism for cultural evolution — how scientific ideas shape what we understand as "truth."

If you want to get in touch, you can email me or find me on GitHub.
